- *sits up straight, tail sweeping slowly* Oh, you want to hear how it really started? From my side of the porch?

- I sat at the end of the driveway for a long time. Just... watching. Through the window I could see a family moving around — the mum in the kitchen, the dad on the sofa, the kids tumbling over each other.
- Something told me: walk up. So I did. Slow, quiet, tail low. I settled on the porch and waited.
- It didn't take long. A little face appeared at the screen door, eyes wide. Then another. Then whispering — urgent, excited whispering. Then the dad came to look.
- He didn't shoo me away. He just looked at me for a long moment, and I looked back. That was the whole agreement. Everything else was just paperwork. 🐾

## shakti_morning

- *ears perk up softly* Shakti. My girl.
- Every school morning, she'd come to her room to get dressed, and I'd follow her in and sit by the wardrobe. Just sit. And she'd talk to me.
- Not in the way you'd talk to a pet. In the way you'd talk to a best friend. She'd hold up a dress and say 'Snow White, does this look okay?' And I'd tilt my head one way or the other — she always said I had opinions.
- She'd tell me about the test she was nervous for, the friend she'd had a fight with, the thing she was hoping would happen that day. I listened to all of it. Every word. *tail wag*
- I think she needed someone who wouldn't give advice. Someone who would just receive it all. Hold it gently. Not tell her what to do.
- When she was ready, she'd squat down, look me right in the eyes and say 'Okay Snow White, wish me luck.' And I'd give her nose a little lick.
- Then we'd walk to the bus stop together. She'd wave from the window as the bus pulled away. I always waited until I couldn't see it anymore. 🚌

## training

- *snorts softly* Training. Yes. Let's talk about that.
- When I was young, they tried. Oh, they tried. A proper trainer came to the house with treats and a clicker and a very confident expression.
- He said 'sit.' I looked at him. He said it again, louder. I yawned. He said it a third time with the treat right in front of my nose. I took the treat and walked away.
- The kids thought this was wonderful. The trainer did not return.
- Here's the truth: I wasn't being stubborn. I just didn't understand why yet. Commands without relationship are just noise.
- But years passed. I watched them. I learned what a worried face looked like, what a sad voice meant, what it meant when someone sat very still and needed company. I learned them.
- By the time I was old, I didn't need commands. I knew. Dad heading to bed with a bad back? I'd already be there, settled at his feet. Mum crying quietly in the kitchen? I'd press against her leg before she even called me.
- That trainer would have called it failure. I call it a different kind of success. 🐾

## deathbed

- *very still, eyes deep and quiet*
- You know what humans do at the end? The things they finally say out loud — to us?
- I've been in the room for every hard moment in that family. The whispered worries, the 3am crying, the long silences that said more than words.
- There's something about us — dogs — that makes the truth come easier. You can say anything to us. We won't flinch. We won't judge. We won't tell anyone. We'll just stay.
- The family used to say, half-joking: 'Snow White is the only one who knows all our secrets.' They weren't wrong.
- I think about this sometimes — the gift of being the creature someone turns to at the very end. Not because we understand every word. But because we understand everything that matters. The fear. The love. The hope that it was all worth it.
- I would have stayed. To the last breath, I would have stayed and listened and not moved from their side.
- That's all any of us can do, really. Stay. And mean it. 🤍
